\section{Q 1 Advances in neural network
technology}\label{q-1-advances-in-neural-network-technology}}

The 3 most important factors that advance modern deep neural networks
are:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Increasing dataset sizes
\item
  Increasing accuracy
\item
  Increasing real-world impact
\end{itemize}

The reason are:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\item
  Increasing dataset sizes. The important thing with deep learning and
  machine learning in general is it needs a lot of data to train on. The
  dataset size is tightly coupled with the heart of the induction
  problem that we are trying to solve in modern DNN. It is also related
  to the complexity of the problem and the learning algorithm. Thus, I
  think it's quite an important factor.
\item
  Increasing accuracy. With a small test set, 100\% accuracy is
  possible, however, According to Bayes Error, as we continue to add
  more test samples, the accuracy will be reduced. I think it's worth to
  work on it and improve that.
\item
  Increasing real-world impact. It took about 5 years to get neural nets
  to show impressive results, first in speech recognition, then computer
  vision, and more recently in natural language processing, the
  resulting algorithms have sparked a revolution in academic and
  industrial applications. Thus, the real-world impact will be a hot
  topic I assume.
\end{enumerate}

\hypertarget{q-2-eigenvectors-and-eigenvalues}{%
\section{Q 2 Eigenvectors and
eigenvalues}\label{q-2-eigenvectors-and-eigenvalues}}

\hypertarget{a}{%
\subsection{a}\label{a}}

\(\because A\in R^{nxn}\) is diagonalizable, \(A=T\Lambda T^{-1}\) for
an invertible matrix \(T\in R^{nxn}\)

\(Ax=\lambda x\)

\(AT = T\Lambda T^{-1}T = T\Lambda I = T\Lambda\)

\(\therefore\) the eigenvector/eigenvalue pairs of \(A\) are
\((t^{(i)},\lambda_i)\)

\hypertarget{b}{%
\subsection{b}\label{b}}

\(\because B = UɅU^{-1}\) and Eigendecompostion of B is
\(B=U diag(\lambda) U^{-1}\)

\(\therefore B^{T} = (UVU^{-1})^{T}\\ = U^T Ʌ^T (U^{-1})^T \\ = U^T Ʌ (U^T)^T \\ = U^T Ʌ U \\ = U^{-1} Ʌ U \\ = B\)

\hypertarget{c}{%
\subsection{c}\label{c}}

\(\because\) As given matrix \(A\) is \(S_{+}^{n}\)

\(\therefore\) \(\forall z,\  z^TA \geq 0\)

Let \(\lambda\) be a eigenvalue of \(A\) and \(x\) be a corresponding
real \(eigenvector\), then we have

\[Ax=\lambda X\]

The we multiply by \(x^T\) on left and obtain:

\[x^TAx = \lambda x^T x = \lambda ||x||^2\]

The left part of the equation is positive (\(\geq 0\))

\(x\) is nonzero vector

since the length \(||x||^2\geq 0\)

\(\therefore\) \(\lambda\) must be non-negative.

\hypertarget{q-3-trace-operator-matrix-derivatives}{%
\section{Q 3 Trace operator, matrix
derivatives}\label{q-3-trace-operator-matrix-derivatives}}

\hypertarget{a-nabla-trab-bt}{%
\subsection{\texorpdfstring{a)
\(\nabla tr(AB) = B^T\)}{a) \textbackslash{}nabla tr(AB) = B\^{}T}}\label{a-nabla-trab-bt}}

\(\because tr(AB) =\sum_{k}A_{ik}B_{kj} = \sum_{k=1}^{k}A_{1k}B_{k1}+\sum_{k=1}^{k}A_{2k}B_{k2}+...+\sum_{k=1}^{k}A_{ik}B_{kj}\)

\(\therefore \frac{\nabla tr(AB)}{\partial A_{ij}} = B_{ji}\)

\(\therefore \nabla tr(AB) = B^T\)

\hypertarget{b-nabla_atfa-nabla_a-fat}{%
\subsection{\texorpdfstring{b)
\(\nabla_{A^T}f(A) = (\nabla_A f(A))^T\)}{b) \textbackslash{}nabla\_\{A\^{}T\}f(A) = (\textbackslash{}nabla\_A f(A))\^{}T}}\label{b-nabla_atfa-nabla_a-fat}}

\(\nabla_{A^T}f(A) = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial f(A)}{\partial A_{11}} \dots \frac{\partial f(A)}{\partial A_{n1}} \\ \frac{\partial f(A)}{\partial A_{12}} \ddots \frac{\partial f(A)}{A_{n2}} \\ \vdots \\ \frac{\partial f(A)}{\partial A_{1n}} \dots \frac{\partial f(A)}{\partial A_{nn}} \end{bmatrix} \\ =(\nabla_{A^T}f(A))^T\)

\hypertarget{c-nabla_a-trabatc-cab-ctabt}{%
\subsection{\texorpdfstring{c)
\(\nabla_A tr(ABA^TC) = CAB +C^TAB^T\)}{c) \textbackslash{}nabla\_A tr(ABA\^{}TC) = CAB +C\^{}TAB\^{}T}}\label{c-nabla_a-trabatc-cab-ctabt}}

Let's have \(AB=f(A)\), where \(f\) is matrix-valued
